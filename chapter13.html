Chapter 13

The Questions We Were Never Allowed to Ask

Kept or Released by Covenant

The silence began long before the questions.

Children notice patterns with remarkable accuracy. They recognize which conversations bring laughter and which conversations cause a room to grow quiet. They learn which opinions receive approval and which subjects disappear before anyone has an opportunity to finish a sentence.

No parent needs to say, "Do not ask."

The atmosphere says enough.

A little boy sits in a wooden pew beside his grandparents and watches a family enter the sanctuary. The visitors look different from everyone else. Their clothing reflects another generation, another neighborhood, another way of living. He waits for the service to begin, expecting someone to welcome them.

Instead, he notices people looking.

Heads turn.

Conversations pause.

Eyes follow strangers toward the back row.

Nothing cruel is spoken. Nothing dramatic happens. The family remains through the final prayer and quietly leaves before anyone learns their names.

On the drive home, the boy asks a simple question.

"Why did everyone keep staring?"

The answer arrives quickly.

"They are different."

No further explanation follows.

Years later another question takes shape.

Why is a television considered worldly while a radio fills the kitchen with gospel music every morning?

The response comes just as quickly.

"That is how we have always believed."

Another year passes.

Why do women always wear long hair?

Why must men always keep theirs short?

Why is one instrument welcomed into worship while another seems to threaten holiness?

The answers become shorter with every question.

"We do not question those things."

Most children eventually stop asking.

Curiosity slowly becomes conformity because belonging is easier than uncertainty.

Entire congregations learn the same lesson.

The pastor is respected.

The elders are trusted.

The traditions are familiar.

Investigation quietly gives way to repetition until memory begins carrying the weight of doctrine.

Nobody notices the change because everyone inherited the same story.

Generations later, the questions return through people rather than ideas.

A lesbian couple enters the sanctuary on an ordinary Sunday morning. They do not arrive carrying protest signs or demands for attention. They carry worn Bibles, whispered prayers, and the same hope shared by every visitor who has ever searched for a church.

Will someone speak to us?

Will anyone ask our names?

Will this become home?

Across the aisle, an elderly woman watches them enter.

She has loved Jesus for seventy years.

She has prepared meals for grieving families, prayed through countless nights for sick children, and quietly paid utility bills for neighbors who never knew where the money came from. Her kindness has shaped an entire congregation.

She has also never known an openly gay couple.

Everything she believes has been inherited through faithful people who sincerely desired to honor God.

For a brief moment, two stories stand only a few feet apart.

Neither story is the enemy.

Neither story is complete.

Both deserve conversation.

Such moments reveal the education many churches never received.

Entire communities became experts in preserving tradition while remaining strangers to history, biblical scholarship, psychology, sociology, and the lived experiences of people beyond their own circles. Isolation created certainty, and certainty slowly convinced sincere believers that unfamiliarity itself represented discernment.

The result reaches far beyond theology.

Questions disappear.

Listening disappears.

Learning disappears.

People begin speaking about neighbors they have never taken the time to know.

Jesus never demonstrated such distance.

Crowds approached Him carrying every imaginable burden. Some possessed respected reputations while others carried public failure. Some understood religious language while others knew only ordinary survival. The Gospels repeatedly present a Savior who chose conversation before conclusion and relationship before assumption.

His confidence never depended upon avoiding people.

His holiness never required isolation.

His authority never diminished through compassion.

Perhaps that explains why so many religious leaders struggled to understand Him.

Their questions focused on categories.

His attention remained fixed upon people.

The modern church stands in a similar place.

Many congregations possess extraordinary devotion, remarkable generosity, and unwavering commitment to prayer. Such qualities deserve gratitude. Every generation builds upon sacrifices made by those who came before, and no honest examination should ignore their faithfulness.

Gratitude, however, cannot replace responsibility.

Every generation must ask whether inherited certainty still reflects the heart of God or merely echoes the customs of another time.

Questions are not acts of rebellion.

Questions become acts of stewardship when they lead believers back to Scripture with humility, historical awareness, intellectual honesty, and compassionate hearts.

The child who wondered about the staring deserves an answer.

The teenager who asks why appearance carries greater weight than kindness deserves an answer.

The visitor who quietly hopes to be known as a person rather than a category deserves an answer.

The church itself deserves an answer.

Perhaps the questions we were never allowed to ask have never threatened the covenant at all.

Perhaps those questions are the very invitation that leads us beyond inherited assumptions and into a deeper understanding of God, of Scripture, and of one another.

Communities willing to ask together often discover something remarkable.

Faith becomes stronger.

Compassion becomes deeper.

Holiness becomes richer.

The sanctuary becomes quieter in all the right ways.

Heads stop turning.

Whispers disappear.

Strangers become neighbors.

Questions become conversations.

The church begins looking less like a place guarded by fear and more like a family gathered around the presence of Jesus, where every person is first recognized by the simple and sacred truth that no tradition can improve and no culture can erase.

Every human being bears the image of God.
