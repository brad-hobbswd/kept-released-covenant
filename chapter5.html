Chapter 5

The Education We Never Received

Kept or Released by Covenant

Every generation inherits two kinds of knowledge.

One arrives through careful study, thoughtful conversation, and a willingness to examine the evidence wherever it leads. The other arrives through repetition, quietly settling into memory until familiarity becomes indistinguishable from truth. Most people never notice the difference because the voices teaching them are trusted, respected, and deeply loved.

The distinction, however, shapes entire communities.

Many churches excel at preserving devotion. Families gather for prayer, memorize Scripture, support one another through loss, and remain remarkably faithful across decades. Such commitment deserves admiration because genuine faithfulness is increasingly rare in a culture that often values convenience above conviction.

Devotion alone, however, has never been a substitute for education.

The Scriptures themselves invite study. Prophets spoke within historical moments. Apostles wrote to real congregations struggling with specific questions. Words carried meanings shaped by language, geography, politics, economics, and culture. Every passage emerged from a living world that deserves careful examination before modern readers confidently declare universal conclusions.

Many believers never receive that invitation.

They inherit answers before learning how to ask questions.

A child hears that long hair belongs to women and short hair belongs to men without exploring the social world of Corinth. Another learns that owning a television represents spiritual compromise without examining the historical fears that accompanied the arrival of mass media. Music becomes divided into categories of holy and unholy without considering how every generation has defined those categories according to its own experience.

The conclusions become permanent while the conversations disappear.

The result is not ignorance born from laziness.

The result is education never offered.

Entire congregations become remarkably knowledgeable about local tradition while remaining unfamiliar with biblical scholarship, historical interpretation, literary context, or the cultural realities surrounding the text they faithfully read every week. Their love for Scripture remains sincere, yet sincerity cannot provide information that was never presented.

Many people mistake this observation for criticism.

Nothing could be further from the truth.

A farmer who never studied Greek may possess extraordinary wisdom. A grandmother who never attended college may understand prayer with a depth that no academic achievement can replace. Formal education does not create spiritual maturity, nor does a degree guarantee humility.

Knowledge and humility must walk together.

Problems emerge when communities reject learning because they fear what learning might reveal.

History records countless moments when education expanded rather than weakened faith. Archaeology illuminated forgotten cities. Ancient manuscripts strengthened confidence in biblical preservation. Linguistic research clarified difficult passages. Historical investigation explained customs that modern readers could never have discovered through translation alone.

Learning repeatedly demonstrated that truth has nothing to fear.

Fear, however, possesses remarkable influence within isolated environments.

Questions become associated with rebellion.

Higher education becomes associated with compromise.

Different interpretations become associated with unbelief.

Curiosity gradually retreats until silence appears safer than investigation.

Young adults eventually leave home carrying convictions they have never examined for themselves. University classrooms, public libraries, and conversations with people from different backgrounds introduce ideas that were never discussed inside familiar church walls. Some discover that faithful Christians around the world worship differently while sharing a profound love for Jesus. Others encounter historical evidence that challenges assumptions accepted since childhood.

Many experience an unexpected crisis.

Their struggle does not begin because they stopped loving God.

Their struggle begins because nobody prepared them for complexity.

Simple answers rarely survive contact with a complicated world. Scripture itself presents tension, poetry, history, prophecy, narrative, wisdom literature, and letters written to communities separated by language and culture from modern readers. Faith capable of engaging such richness grows stronger through study rather than weaker.

The absence of education carries another cost rarely discussed.

Communities become less equipped to welcome people whose stories differ from their own.

A lesbian woman who has spent years studying Scripture arrives carrying questions formed through prayer and scholarship. A transgender believer speaks thoughtfully about personal faith and biblical interpretation. A recovering addict describes trauma, psychology, and spiritual restoration with language unfamiliar to those who have never encountered such experiences. Immigrant families bring traditions shaped by cultures older than the congregation itself.

Conversations quickly become difficult when one side has never been encouraged to explore beyond inherited assumptions.

Listening requires education.

Empathy requires education.

Historical understanding requires education.

The ability to separate personal discomfort from biblical conviction requires education.

Such learning does not demand abandoning deeply held beliefs. Careful study often strengthens conviction by removing fear and replacing certainty built upon repetition with confidence built upon understanding.

Perhaps that is why Jesus spent so much of His ministry teaching.

He asked questions that unsettled comfortable assumptions. He challenged interpretations that had become untouchable. He invited listeners to look beyond familiar traditions and discover the heart of God hidden beneath centuries of accumulated expectation.

Education became an act of liberation.

People who once accepted inherited conclusions without examination suddenly found themselves thinking, searching, comparing, and discovering.

That invitation remains just as necessary today.

The church cannot faithfully engage the modern world while refusing to understand the modern world. Congregations cannot proclaim hospitality while remaining unfamiliar with the experiences of those standing at the sanctuary door. Believers cannot confidently distinguish covenant from culture without first learning how both have shaped the faith they inherited.

The purpose of education is never to create doubt for its own sake.

The purpose of education is to cultivate disciples who love God with heart, soul, strength, and mind, possessing the humility to learn, the courage to ask, and the wisdom to recognize that every generation must return to Scripture with fresh eyes and an open spirit.

Only then can faith become an informed conviction rather than an inherited certainty, capable of welcoming every sincere seeker without fear that an honest question might somehow diminish the truth.
