Chapter 14

The Weight of Looking Holy

Kept or Released by Covenant

Every community creates symbols.

Some symbols inspire hope. A cross rising above a sanctuary reminds weary people that death never received the final word. An open Bible resting upon a pulpit declares that generations have gathered to hear something greater than themselves. A baptismal pool celebrates new life and new beginnings.

Other symbols emerge quietly.

A sleeve length.

A hairstyle.

A skirt hem.

A clean shave.

A particular instrument.

A particular translation.

A particular way of speaking.

None of these symbols begin as enemies of faith. Most begin as sincere expressions of conviction, reflecting the desire of ordinary believers to live lives set apart for God.

Time changes the meaning.

Practices once embraced as personal convictions gradually become public expectations. The expectation becomes a measurement, and the measurement quietly assumes the authority of Scripture.

The transformation happens so slowly that almost no one notices.

A young woman enters church wearing a simple dress that reaches her knees.

She believes she has dressed modestly.

An older member notices the difference immediately.

Nothing is said.

No correction follows.

The silence itself becomes instruction.

The young woman returns home wondering whether she disappointed God without understanding why.

Across town, a young man allows his hair to grow below his ears.

He still prays every morning.

He still studies Scripture.

He still volunteers at the local shelter every Saturday.

People begin discussing his appearance long before anyone asks about his character.

Another church welcomes a family that recently moved into the community.

Their son has tattoos.

Their daughter wears bright colors.

Their grandchildren laugh too loudly during fellowship.

Within a week, everyone knows what they look like.

Almost no one knows their names.

Appearance possesses extraordinary power because appearance requires no effort.

Character demands relationship.

The easiest judgment is often the least informed.

Many believers spend years carrying invisible exhaustion.

Every service becomes another opportunity to perform familiarity.

Every conversation becomes another opportunity to avoid saying the wrong thing.

Every decision is filtered through an unspoken question.

Will this make someone question my spirituality?

The burden grows heavier than anyone intended.

Holiness slowly becomes associated with anxiety rather than joy.

Jesus never described such a life.

His invitations consistently moved toward freedom rather than performance.

He spoke of rest for weary souls.

He spoke of truth that liberates.

He spoke of burdens made lighter rather than heavier.

Religious communities, however, possess an extraordinary ability to create expectations that multiply until ordinary believers begin carrying weights God never asked them to bear.

The pattern reaches beyond clothing.

Churches develop acceptable personalities.

Some emotions become respectable while others become suspicious.

Confidence appears rebellious.

Grief appears weak.

Questions appear dangerous.

Difference appears threatening.

People learn to present carefully edited versions of themselves, believing authenticity may cost them acceptance.

The congregation grows remarkably uniform.

Uniformity creates comfort.

Comfort often disguises distance.

The church begins celebrating appearances while quietly overlooking loneliness sitting three pews away.

A transgender woman faithfully attends every Wednesday evening Bible study.

She never misses a prayer request.

She volunteers whenever meals are prepared for struggling families.

Many know her schedule.

Very few know her story.

A gay man spends years serving quietly in community outreach projects.

He helps repair homes damaged by storms, delivers groceries to elderly neighbors, and donates blood every opportunity he receives.

His generosity becomes familiar.

His humanity remains unexplored.

People become remarkably easy to categorize whenever relationship is absent.

The Gospels reveal another way.

Jesus repeatedly encountered individuals already defined by society.

Tax collector.

Sinner.

Leper.

Foreigner.

Woman.

Child.

Every label carried assumptions.

Jesus consistently looked beyond the label before speaking.

He recognized people hidden beneath public identity.

Perhaps that explains why so many ordinary men and women found themselves drawn toward Him while religious certainty often remained uncomfortable in His presence.

Love possesses remarkable vision.

Love notices tears before appearance.

Love notices loneliness before reputation.

Love notices hunger before presentation.

The church loses something precious whenever visible conformity becomes the primary evidence of spiritual life.

Compassion slowly retreats.

Curiosity disappears.

Hospitality becomes carefully managed rather than freely given.

The sanctuary remains full, yet conversation becomes increasingly narrow because everyone has learned the same lesson.

Looking holy is safer than being known.

The covenant invites another possibility.

Imagine a congregation where questions carry no shame.

Imagine elders who listen before correcting.

Imagine young people who discover that education strengthens faith rather than threatening it.

Imagine visitors whose first memory of church is not the eyes that followed them across the sanctuary but the hands that welcomed them to lunch afterward.

Such communities already exist.

They are rarely defined by perfect agreement.

They are remembered for remarkable grace.

Holiness flourishes there as well, though holiness looks surprisingly familiar to the ministry of Jesus.

The hungry are fed.

The lonely become friends.

The forgotten receive names instead of labels.

The stranger becomes family.

Perhaps genuine holiness has always been less concerned with appearing different from the world and more concerned with loving the world in a way that reflects the heart of God.

Such holiness cannot be measured by a mirror.

Such holiness reveals itself every time a congregation chooses relationship over assumption and every time a person discovers that the presence of Jesus weighs far more than the expectations of culture.
