Chapter 14

Who Told Us That?

Kept or Released by Covenant

Every belief has a history.

Some arrive through careful study of Scripture, shaped by language, context, and centuries of thoughtful examination. Others arrive much more quietly. They are repeated at family tables, reinforced from pulpits, and carried from one generation to the next until nobody remembers where they began.

The belief simply exists.

The question never comes.

Who told us that?

For many people, childhood faith resembles an old family photograph. Every face is familiar, every tradition comforting, and every memory wrapped in the certainty that everyone gathered together understood God in exactly the same way.

No one questioned why women wore their hair long.

No one questioned why television remained outside the home while a radio playing southern gospel music occupied a place of honor in the kitchen.

No one questioned why one instrument belonged in worship while another seemed capable of inviting spiritual disaster.

No one questioned why a man with longer hair appeared rebellious before speaking a single word.

Questions belonged to another world.

Obedience belonged to this one.

Looking back, such certainty carried a remarkable innocence.

Most families were not raising theologians.

They were raising children.

Parents relied upon pastors they trusted. Pastors relied upon leaders who had shaped them decades earlier. Those leaders often relied upon interpretations inherited from still another generation. The chain stretched backward through time until local custom became almost impossible to separate from biblical conviction.

Nobody intended to deceive anyone.

Most sincerely believed they were protecting something precious.

The twentieth century brought extraordinary change to American churches. New forms of entertainment entered homes. Cities expanded. Families left farms for factories and offices. Television introduced ideas that many believers found unsettling. Popular culture challenged long established values, and churches responded by building visible boundaries designed to preserve spiritual identity.

The boundaries made sense within their moment.

A television represented more than furniture.

Long hair represented more than style.

Music represented more than melody.

Each became a symbol, reassuring believers that they remained different from a rapidly changing world.

Symbols, however, possess an unusual tendency.

They survive long after the original circumstances disappear.

The grandchildren inherit the boundary without inheriting the reason.

Eventually, someone asks an innocent question.

Where is that found in Scripture?

The room becomes quiet.

Not because the question lacks value.

The silence appears because nobody has been taught how to answer.

Many churches confused confidence with certainty.

Confidence says, "This is how we have understood this passage."

Certainty declares, "No faithful Christian could possibly understand this differently."

Education creates the difference.

A student of history learns that faithful believers have disagreed throughout every century of Christian thought. Scholars who love Scripture examine language, archaeology, literature, and culture before reaching conclusions. Entire libraries exist because sincere people devoted their lives to understanding the biblical world with greater accuracy.

Learning does not weaken conviction.

Learning refines conviction.

Perhaps that explains why some of the strongest believers become the most humble.

The more they study, the more they recognize the extraordinary complexity of Scripture and the remarkable faithfulness of God throughout history.

Humility begins replacing fear.

Conversation replaces assumption.

Curiosity becomes an act of worship rather than an act of rebellion.

A church living in that spirit approaches newcomers differently.

A transgender visitor is no longer viewed as evidence of cultural decline but as a person carrying joys, wounds, disappointments, questions, and a desire to know God.

A lesbian couple becomes two human beings rather than a headline.

A recovering addict becomes a testimony unfolding rather than a cautionary tale.

The homeless man sleeping beneath a bridge becomes a neighbor instead of a problem.

Education changes vision.

People begin seeing stories where they once saw stereotypes.

Such transformation does not require abandoning Scripture.

The opposite is true.

Returning to Scripture often reveals how many assumptions quietly attached themselves to the text through generations of repetition. Local customs became universal principles. Regional culture borrowed the language of covenant. Familiar practices acquired the authority of commands God never actually gave.

Recognizing that reality should produce gratitude rather than embarrassment.

The grandparents who refused to own a television were often the same people who opened their homes to strangers, prayed through impossible circumstances, and gave sacrificially when they possessed almost nothing themselves. Their devotion deserves admiration.

Their interpretations deserve examination.

Those two truths comfortably occupy the same page.

Every generation receives a sacred responsibility.

Honor the faith that brought you here.

Study deeply enough to know why you believe what you believe.

Refuse the arrogance that assumes previous generations understood everything perfectly.

Refuse the arrogance that assumes your generation does.

Keep learning.

Keep listening.

Keep returning to Scripture.

Perhaps the most liberating question a believer can ever ask is also the simplest.

Who told me that?

The answer may lead to a parent, a pastor, a denomination, a beloved grandparent, or a childhood church filled with sincere people doing the best they could with the understanding they possessed.

The next question matters even more.

What does Scripture reveal when I approach it with humility, historical awareness, intellectual honesty, and a heart that longs to know God more than it longs to defend tradition?

Every reformation in church history began with that question.

Perhaps every personal reformation begins there as well.
