Chapter 8

Becoming Like Everyone Else

Kept or Released by Covenant

Every community possesses a quiet understanding of what acceptance looks like.

Some expectations are spoken with remarkable clarity. Others exist beneath the surface, guiding behavior through observation rather than instruction. Newcomers quickly learn that belonging often depends upon mastering an unwritten language, one communicated through appearance, vocabulary, music, friendships, and countless social cues that longtime members scarcely notice anymore.

The process appears harmless until acceptance becomes conditional.

A visitor arrives searching for God and gradually discovers that discipleship seems to require something more. Faith alone does not appear sufficient. Looking the part, speaking the right phrases, avoiding certain people, embracing familiar opinions, and adopting generations of inherited customs quietly become signs of spiritual maturity.

Transformation gives way to imitation.

The distinction deserves careful attention.

Scripture repeatedly speaks about becoming more like Christ. Communities, however, often encourage people to become more like themselves. The difference may appear insignificant at first, yet the consequences reach into every corner of church life.

One path invites growth.

The other demands conformity.

Conformity offers remarkable comfort because uniformity creates the appearance of unity. Everyone dresses similarly, worships similarly, votes similarly, and interprets experience through nearly identical assumptions. Conflict becomes less visible because diversity has quietly disappeared.

Such environments often mistake sameness for holiness.

The earliest followers of Jesus never enjoyed that luxury.

Their communities gathered fishermen and tax collectors, wealthy merchants and laborers, Jews and Gentiles, women of influence and men with no social standing, scholars and ordinary workers. Languages differed. Customs differed. Histories differed. Their unity emerged not from cultural similarity but from a shared commitment to God.

The covenant created belonging before uniformity.

Modern congregations sometimes reverse that order.

People are expected to resemble the community before the community learns who they are. The pressure remains subtle yet unmistakable. Clothing changes first. Language changes next. Personal interests slowly disappear beneath expectations inherited from previous generations. Questions become quieter. Individual stories become less visible. Authenticity yields to performance because performance appears safer than honesty.

Many believers spend years becoming acceptable while quietly wondering whether anyone truly knows them.

The emotional cost remains largely invisible.

A young woman cuts away pieces of her personality because confidence has been mistaken for rebellion. A teenager learns to hide artistic gifts that fail to fit established expectations. A divorced father avoids conversation about his past because previous experiences taught him that vulnerability invites judgment.

The same pattern reaches people whose lives challenge familiar assumptions.

A gay man quickly learns which conversations must remain silent if he hopes to continue attending without becoming the subject of speculation.

A lesbian couple discovers that friendliness often disappears when personal history becomes known.

A transgender person carefully measures every word, every gesture, and every interaction, hoping sincere faith will be recognized before identity overshadows humanity.

Silence becomes a survival strategy.

Performance becomes a form of protection.

Communities rarely intend to create such burdens. Most believe they are preserving biblical faithfulness while encouraging spiritual growth. The desire itself may be sincere, yet sincerity cannot erase the difference between inviting people into covenant and inviting them into culture.

Jesus consistently resisted that confusion.

Crowds approached Him carrying remarkably different histories, occupations, reputations, and failures. Some possessed extensive religious education while others knew only ordinary labor and daily survival. He never required anyone to adopt the customs of another person before extending dignity, conversation, or compassion.

His invitation always preceded transformation.

Relationship came before restoration.

People encountered grace before understanding the full depth of discipleship.

That pattern challenged religious expectations in the first century and continues to challenge them today.

The church flourishes whenever people discover freedom to bring their whole story into the presence of God. Honest questions replace rehearsed answers. Testimonies become richer because struggle no longer requires concealment. Worship gains authenticity because gratitude grows from genuine experience rather than public performance.

Such communities remain deeply committed to holiness.

Their understanding of holiness, however, begins with the character of God rather than the preferences of culture. Compassion becomes evidence of faithfulness. Hospitality becomes an act of worship. Humility becomes the natural response to the recognition that every believer stands before God through grace rather than personal achievement.

Perhaps the greatest danger facing any congregation is not diversity.

Perhaps the greatest danger is believing that spiritual maturity can be measured by how successfully people disappear into the expectations of the majority.

The kingdom of God has always been larger than a single culture, a single tradition, or a single expression of worship. Every generation receives the responsibility of examining whether it is creating disciples shaped by the heart of God or merely producing individuals skilled at resembling one another.

That examination requires courage.

Communities built upon imitation may preserve appearances for many years, yet communities built upon covenant possess something far more enduring. Such communities create space for truth, growth, repentance, education, and compassion to exist together without fear.

Every person entering the sanctuary deserves that freedom.

Every child deserves to inherit a faith strong enough to welcome questions.

Every newcomer deserves the opportunity to encounter God before learning the customs of the room.

Every congregation deserves the opportunity to discover that unity has never depended upon becoming identical. Unity has always depended upon recognizing the sacred worth of every person gathered beneath the same promise, seeking the same God, and longing for the same grace that first welcomed every one of us.
