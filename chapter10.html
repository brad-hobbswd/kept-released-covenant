Chapter 10

The Church We Built and the Church Jesus Builds

Kept or Released by Covenant

Every generation inherits a choice.

Some inherit buildings that have stood for more than a century, their walls holding the echoes of sermons, weddings, funerals, and prayers whispered through seasons of war, economic hardship, revival, and loss. Others inherit a small congregation gathered inside a renovated storefront or a fellowship hall where folding chairs replace wooden pews.

Buildings are inherited.

Culture is inherited as well.

The difference is that culture continues to grow every time people stop asking why.

Many churches begin with a simple desire to follow God faithfully. Men and women sacrifice their time, their finances, and their comfort to establish communities where worship can flourish and families can grow together in faith. They possess no hidden agenda, no desire to exclude, and no intention of creating unnecessary burdens.

They simply build according to what they know.

The next generation receives those foundations without witnessing their construction.

Rules appear without history.

Expectations exist without explanation.

Practices become permanent because nobody remembers the circumstances that first created them.

The church slowly forgets the difference between preserving the gospel and preserving itself.

A congregation that once opened its doors to every searching soul gradually becomes more concerned with protecting identity than extending hospitality. Familiarity begins replacing curiosity. Tradition quietly occupies the space where education should have been encouraged.

The transformation happens so slowly that faithful people scarcely recognize it.

The church continues singing the same hymns.

The prayers remain sincere.

Scripture is still read every Sunday.

Compassion, however, becomes increasingly selective.

A homeless family receives assistance but never quite receives friendship.

A divorced couple is welcomed until their history becomes public knowledge.

A teenager struggling with identity discovers that conversations become uncomfortable the moment honesty enters the room.

A gay man faithfully attends worship for months, yet every sermon referencing people like him speaks about an issue rather than a person.

A transgender woman volunteers quietly in the community, serves food to neighbors, donates clothing to local families, and remains the last person to leave every outreach event. Many still know her only through assumptions formed before a single conversation ever takes place.

The church often wonders why people leave.

Those who leave frequently wonder whether they were ever invited to stay.

Such stories deserve more than quick explanations.

People rarely abandon faith because of one sermon or one disagreement. More often, departure begins through countless small moments that accumulate over years. Every unanswered question, every suspicious glance, every whispered conversation, and every assumption communicated without words slowly convinces a person that belonging will always remain beyond reach.

Many continue loving God while quietly walking away from communities that never learned to love them.

The loss belongs to everyone.

The congregation loses voices that would have expanded understanding.

Children lose mentors whose life experiences differ from their own.

Friendships never develop because stereotypes end conversations before introductions begin.

The community grows increasingly uniform while believing such uniformity reflects spiritual success.

Jesus consistently disrupted that pattern.

His ministry attracted people who shared almost nothing in common except their desire to be near Him. Fishermen worshiped beside tax collectors. Women whose reputations had become public scandal found dignity alongside respected religious leaders. The poor stood beside the wealthy. The educated listened beside those who could neither read nor write.

No single culture defined the kingdom He proclaimed.

No preferred social class determined who belonged.

No outward appearance established worth.

Every person entered through the same invitation.

Perhaps that reality explains why so many religious leaders found His ministry unsettling. The boundaries they carefully maintained became increasingly difficult to defend in the presence of radical compassion. Their certainty depended upon separation. His ministry flourished through relationship.

The modern church faces the same decision.

One path continues measuring faithfulness by familiarity, rewarding those who naturally fit inherited expectations while quietly overlooking those whose stories challenge comfortable assumptions.

The other path requires courage.

Such courage asks a congregation to know people before defining them.

Such courage welcomes education instead of fearing it.

Such courage admits that previous generations possessed extraordinary devotion while remaining capable of misunderstanding certain passages, historical contexts, and cultural realities.

Such courage believes that truth grows stronger through honest examination.

None of these observations diminish the beauty of holiness.

Holiness remains central to the Christian life.

Holiness, however, has always been concerned with the transformation of the heart before the management of appearance. A compassionate spirit, a commitment to justice, a love for mercy, a willingness to forgive, and a humble walk with God reveal a holiness that reaches beyond clothing, hairstyles, entertainment choices, or social categories.

The church Jesus builds never fears the arrival of unfamiliar people.

The church Jesus builds expects them.

Every empty seat carries the possibility that someone carrying grief, addiction, poverty, rejection, doubt, or isolation will gather enough courage to walk through the front doors. Every worship service presents another opportunity to embody the covenant rather than merely discuss it.

The measure of a congregation will never be found in the sameness of the people filling the pews.

The measure will be found in whether every person who enters discovers a community willing to listen, learn, and love before reaching conclusions.

That calling demands more than tradition.

That calling demands more than certainty.

That calling demands hearts willing to recognize that the kingdom of God has always been far larger than the boundaries humanity has attempted to draw around it.

Only then does the church become what it was always intended to be, not a museum preserving a particular culture, but a living community where every searching soul encounters the grace, truth, and transforming presence of Jesus.
