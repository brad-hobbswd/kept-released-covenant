Chapter 7

The People We Learned to Fear

Kept or Released by Covenant

Fear rarely announces itself as fear.

Fear often introduces itself as wisdom, caution, discernment, or the desire to protect what is sacred. Such language sounds responsible and even spiritual, making fear remarkably difficult to recognize once it has settled into the life of a community. Generations inherit those instincts without ever realizing they are carrying burdens that did not originate with Scripture.

The process begins innocently enough.

Parents teach children to be careful. Pastors encourage congregations to remain separate from influences that might weaken faith. Families develop routines designed to preserve values they believe are under constant attack. Every decision appears motivated by love and concern.

Years pass.

The circle quietly grows smaller.

The unfamiliar becomes suspicious.

Difference becomes dangerous.

Eventually, entire groups of people become known not by their names but by labels carefully repeated until humanity disappears behind them.

The church begins speaking about people it has never taken the time to know.

A congregation may discuss the homeless without sharing a meal with someone living on the street. Members may debate addiction without sitting beside a recovering alcoholic who has spent months rebuilding a shattered life. Conversations about gay, lesbian, and transgender people often unfold among individuals who have never listened to the testimony of someone who spent years praying, searching Scripture, and longing for a place to worship without fear.

Distance allows assumptions to flourish.

Relationship exposes assumptions for what they are.

Many believers discover this truth unexpectedly.

A teacher meets a student whose kindness challenges every stereotype inherited from childhood. A pastor sits beside a grieving parent whose family story refuses to fit neatly inside familiar categories. A church volunteer serves meals alongside people whose generosity exceeds that of many lifelong members. Suddenly, inherited certainty encounters living humanity.

The conversation changes.

Abstract issues become real people.

Political language gives way to personal stories.

Fear begins losing the authority it once possessed.

Communities built upon limited experience often mistake unfamiliarity for discernment. Every person who looks different, dresses differently, speaks differently, or identifies differently appears to represent a threat rather than an opportunity for relationship. Such instincts rarely develop from careful study. More often they emerge from isolation, repetition, and generations of inherited caution.

Education changes that landscape.

History reminds believers that every era has identified a different group as the outsider. One generation feared immigrants. Another feared racial integration. Others feared higher education, new forms of music, women in leadership, or believers from different denominations. Each period believed its concerns uniquely justified, yet time repeatedly revealed how easily fear disguises itself as faithfulness.

The pattern deserves careful attention because the church has often confused separation from sin with separation from people.

Jesus never demonstrated such confusion.

His ministry unfolded in homes, marketplaces, streets, and gatherings filled with individuals religious society preferred to avoid. His critics consistently objected to the company He kept rather than the truth He proclaimed. They questioned His willingness to share meals, engage conversation, and extend dignity to people they considered spiritually unacceptable.

Their discomfort revealed more about their expectations than about His character.

Love possesses remarkable courage.

Love listens before speaking.

Love refuses to reduce another human being to a single characteristic or identity.

Love recognizes that every person carries experiences invisible to casual observation.

The teenager quietly entering church after months of bullying deserves more than suspicion.

The lesbian couple searching for a community where prayer feels possible deserves more than whispered conversations.

The transgender adult who has spent years believing God could never love them deserves more than a curious glance across the sanctuary.

The elderly widow living in poverty deserves more than polite sympathy.

The former prisoner deserves more than a permanent reminder of yesterday.

Every person deserves to be seen as fully human before becoming the subject of theological discussion.

Such recognition does not erase conviction.

Rather, conviction becomes stronger when rooted in relationship instead of assumption.

Communities willing to know people before categorizing them discover an unexpected gift. Fear gradually loses its influence because understanding replaces imagination. Stories replace stereotypes. Conversations replace conclusions. The unfamiliar slowly becomes familiar, and neighbors emerge where strangers once stood.

Many churches have invested extraordinary energy protecting themselves from people outside their walls while overlooking a quieter danger growing within them.

Isolation.

Isolation limits compassion.

Isolation narrows perspective.

Isolation convinces sincere believers that their experience represents the entire world.

The covenant revealed through Jesus continually moves in another direction. Such a covenant reaches outward instead of inward. Every barrier becomes an invitation to cross the room, extend a hand, ask a name, and discover a story that cannot be summarized by appearance alone.

Perhaps the greatest lesson awaiting the modern church is wonderfully simple.

The people we learned to fear are often the very people waiting to teach us humility, compassion, resilience, and grace.

Only after fear releases its grip can a congregation begin seeing what God has always seen.

Not categories.

Not controversies.

Not labels.

People.

People carrying hope, sorrow, faith, doubt, memory, longing, and the unmistakable imprint of the One who created every human soul with immeasurable worth.

Such a realization does not weaken the covenant.

Such a realization reveals the extraordinary breadth of a covenant large enough to invite every searching heart through the same open door.
